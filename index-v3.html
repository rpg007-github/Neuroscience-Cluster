Charting Our Course

Six Lines of Inquiry

Every great voyage begins with a chart. Ours spans molecule to community.

Neurodegeneration & Alzheimer's Disease

Investigating calpain-mediated tau proteolysis, phage display biomarker discovery, and the spatial epidemiology of Alzheimer's mortality across the United States. More than 30 years of continuous NIH funding.

Rodney Guttmann, Ph.D.

Computational Neuroscience & Neural Dynamics

Decoding prefrontal cortex activity during working memory using machine learning, stochastic modeling of ion channel noise, and mathematical frameworks for how neural networks encode information.

Shusen Pu, Ph.D.

Cellular & Molecular Signaling

Characterizing HAX1 and HS1 cytoskeletal dynamics in neutrophils, neuro-immune interactions via dopamine receptor signaling, and antipsychotic drug effects — with direct implications for neurological disease.

Peter Cavnar, Ph.D.

Developmental & Cognitive Neuroscience

Using EEG to study sensory processing in ASD and ADHD, exploring visual working memory in applied settings, and investigating how bilingualism and executive function interact across the lifespan.

Rainey, Ph.D. & Blalock, Ph.D.

Exercise Neuroscience & Neuroprotection

Elucidating how endurance exercise protects the brain through autophagy and mitophagy pathways — with direct applications to Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease prevention and metabolic-distress-induced neurodegeneration.

Youngil Lee, Ph.D.

Stroke & Brain Health Disparities

Examining racial, geographic, and socioeconomic disparities in stroke mortality across Florida and the nation, studying stroke education interventions in Black and Hispanic communities, and tracking healthcare equity among survivors.

Daudet Ilunga Tshiswaka, Ph.D.

Meet Our Investigators

Seven faculty from four UWF colleges, united by a shared passion for brain science.

RG
Cluster Director • Principal Investigator, NeuroNauts

Rodney Guttmann, Ph.D.

Professor & Interim Chair, Biology • Hal Marcus College of Science and Engineering

Dr. Guttmann has dedicated more than 30 years to Alzheimer's disease research, maintaining continuous NIH funding throughout his career. His laboratory pioneered phage display approaches to biomarker discovery and has contributed landmark findings on calpain-mediated tau proteolysis. A two-term department chair and former director of UWF's Center on Aging, he has mentored more than 100 undergraduate researchers and hosted a weekly NPR aging segment for over a decade. He holds two U.S. patents.

Alzheimer's Disease Tau Biology Spatial Epidemiology Biomarker Discovery

rguttmann@uwf.edu

SP
Co-Founder • Computational Lead

Shusen Pu, Ph.D.

Assistant Professor, Mathematics & Statistics • Hal Marcus College of Science and Engineering

An applied mathematician turned neuroscientist, Dr. Pu develops stochastic models and machine learning frameworks to decode how the prefrontal cortex encodes working memory. His work — published in Nature Communications and Neural Computation — bridges the gap between individual neuron dynamics and large-scale cognitive function. A 2024 Faculty Excellence in Undergraduate Research Mentoring Award recipient and 2024 SGA Distinguished Teaching Award winner, he mentors more than 30 students in the Computational Statistics and Data Analytics Lab, and served as a plenary speaker at the 2025 Joint Annual Meetings of the MAA-Florida Section.

Computational Neuroscience Machine Learning Working Memory Stochastic Modeling

spu@uwf.edu  •  shusenpu.com

PC
Co-Founder • Cell Biology

Peter Cavnar, Ph.D.

Associate Professor & Associate Dean • Hal Marcus College of Science and Engineering

Dr. Cavnar's research sits at the intersection of cell biology and neuroscience, investigating how cytoskeletal regulatory proteins like HAX1 and HS1 govern cell motility and survival, and how dopamine receptor signaling in neutrophils illuminates neuro-immune pathways relevant to psychiatric and neurological disease. He has mentored more than 35 undergraduate and graduate students and co-led UWF's NIH U-RISE program for a decade. As former Chair of Biology and a Howard Hughes Medical Institute Scientific Teaching Fellow, he has fundamentally reshaped undergraduate science education at UWF.

Cytoskeletal Dynamics Neuro-Immune Interactions Neutrophil Biology Antipsychotic Pharmacology

pcavnar@uwf.edu

DT
Co-Founder • Public Health Neuroscience

Daudet Ilunga Tshiswaka, Ph.D.

Associate Professor, Public Health • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Tshiswaka examines the social and structural forces that drive disparities in stroke mortality, healthcare access, and brain health outcomes — particularly in Black, Hispanic, and immigrant communities. A Visiting Assistant Professor at Columbia University's Stroke Division from 2019 to 2023 and former Acting Chair of Public Health at UWF, he has led NIH/NINDS-funded research on stroke literacy interventions and health equity across Florida's Panhandle. His work has appeared in Stroke (AHA), Preventing Chronic Disease (CDC), and the Journal of Public Health.

Stroke Disparities Health Equity Community Health Immigrant Health

dilunga@uwf.edu

VR
Cognitive & Developmental Neuroscience

Vanessa Rainey, Ph.D.

Associate Professor, Psychology • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Rainey investigates sensory processing differences in autism spectrum disorder and ADHD using EEG, examining the neural underpinnings of attention, working memory, and language development. She explores how home environments, bilingualism, and cultural factors shape brain development across childhood and adolescence. Her work has been published in Scholarship of Teaching and Learning in Psychology, the International Journal of Psychophysiology, and Psychophysiology. She is co-authoring a major revision of a human development textbook (MacMillan) and is a committed champion of high-impact undergraduate mentorship.

ASD & ADHD EEG/Neuroimaging Sensory Processing Language & Cognition

vrainey@uwf.edu  •  cogdevlab.uwf.edu

LB
Applied Cognition & Visual Memory

Lisa Blalock, Ph.D.

Professor & Chair, Psychology • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Blalock's research centers on visual working memory — how people store, maintain, and retrieve visual information — and how training can build visual expertise. Using rigorous behavioral experiments, she investigates encoding, maintenance, and retrieval with applications ranging from eyewitness testimony reliability to educational design and user interface usability. She is a contributor to the forthcoming Oxford Handbook of Undergraduate Psychology Education (2nd edition) and is known for pedagogical innovation that translates cognitive science into real-world strategies.

Visual Working Memory Applied Cognition Visual Expertise Educational Psychology

lblalock@uwf.edu

YL
Exercise Neuroscience

Youngil Lee, Ph.D.

Professor, Kinesiology • Hal Marcus College of Science and Engineering

Dr. Lee investigates how endurance exercise protects the brain and heart through autophagy and mitophagy — the cellular processes that clear dysfunctional proteins and organelles. His research demonstrates that regular physical activity can mitigate neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer's and Parkinson's, and counteract metabolic-distress-induced senescence in the hippocampus. Published in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, the Journal of Molecular Neuroscience, and Life Sciences, his work has direct translational implications for aging brain health. He is a 2024 to 2027 Reubin O'D. Askew Institute for Multidiscipline Studies Fellow at UWF.

Exercise Neuroprotection Autophagy/Mitophagy Neurodegeneration Prevention Metabolic Disease

ylee@uwf.edu

NeuroNauts Scholars Program

An intensive, NIH-funded summer research experience that brings high school students into working neuroscience laboratories at UWF.

This is not a camp. NeuroNauts scholars work side-by-side with undergraduate and graduate researchers in one of our six cluster laboratories — designing experiments, collecting and analyzing real data, presenting findings, and leaving with a scientific identity that sets them apart.

  • Hands-on research in active NIH-funded laboratories
  • Mentorship from faculty, graduate, and undergraduate researchers
  • Training across EEG, cell culture, computational modeling, behavioral experiments, and community health methods
  • Weekly journal clubs and scientific communication workshops
  • Final symposium presentation for faculty and family
