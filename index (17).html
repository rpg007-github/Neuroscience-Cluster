The UWF Neuroscience Research Cluster unites faculty from biology, mathematics, psychology, and public health to investigate the brain and nervous system from complementary angles — molecular mechanisms, computational dynamics, cognitive behavior, and population-level health disparities.

What We Study

Five Pillars of Inquiry

Our investigators share a commitment to brain science that matters — translatable from cell to society.

Neurodegeneration & Alzheimer's Disease

Investigating post-translational modifications of tau, calpain-mediated proteolysis, phage display biomarker discovery, and spatial epidemiology of Alzheimer's mortality clustering across the United States.

Led by: Rodney Guttmann, Ph.D.

Computational Neuroscience & Neural Dynamics

Decoding prefrontal cortex activity during working memory using machine learning, stochastic modeling of ion channel noise, and mathematical frameworks for understanding how neural networks encode and maintain information.

Led by: Shusen Pu, Ph.D.

Cellular & Molecular Signaling

Characterizing HAX1 and HS1 cytoskeletal dynamics in neutrophils, neuro-immune interactions via dopamine receptor signaling, and antipsychotic drug effects on cell behavior — with direct implications for neurological disease.

Led by: Peter Cavnar, Ph.D.

Developmental & Cognitive Neuroscience

Using EEG to study sensory processing in autism spectrum disorder and ADHD, exploring visual working memory and attention in applied settings, and investigating how bilingualism and executive function interact across development.

Led by: Vanessa Rainey, Ph.D. & Lisa Blalock, Ph.D.

Exercise Neuroscience & Neuroprotection

Elucidating how endurance exercise protects the brain through autophagy and mitophagy pathways, with applications to Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease prevention, and metabolic distress-induced neurodegeneration in women.

Led by: Youngil Lee, Ph.D.

Stroke & Brain Health Disparities

Examining racial, geographic, and socioeconomic disparities in stroke mortality across Florida and the nation, studying stroke education interventions in Black and Hispanic communities, and tracking healthcare access among stroke survivors.

Led by: Daudet Ilunga Tshiswaka, Ph.D.

The Investigators

Meet Our Team

Six faculty from across UWF's colleges, united by a shared passion for brain science.

RG
Cluster Director • PI, NeuroNauts

Rodney Guttmann, Ph.D.

Professor & Interim Chair, Biology • HMCSE

Dr. Guttmann has dedicated more than 30 years to Alzheimer's disease research, maintaining continuous NIH funding across his career. His laboratory pioneered phage display approaches to biomarker discovery and has contributed landmark findings on calpain-mediated tau proteolysis. A two-term department chair and former director of UWF's Center on Aging, he has mentored more than 100 undergraduate researchers and hosted a weekly NPR aging segment ("The Senior Moment") for over a decade. He holds two U.S. patents and serves as Interim CEO and Board Chair of Covenant Care, a major Northwest Florida healthcare nonprofit.

  • Alzheimer's Disease
  • Tau Biology
  • Spatial Epidemiology
  • Biomarker Discovery

rguttmann@uwf.edu

SP
Co-Founder • Computational Lead

Shusen Pu, Ph.D.

Assistant Professor, Mathematics & Statistics • HMCSE

An applied mathematician turned neuroscientist, Dr. Pu develops stochastic models and machine learning frameworks to decode how the prefrontal cortex encodes working memory. His work — published in Nature Communications and Neural Computation — bridges the gap between individual neuron dynamics and large-scale cognitive function. A 2024 Faculty Excellence in Undergraduate Research Mentoring Award recipient and 2024 SGA Distinguished Teaching Award winner, he mentors more than 30 students in the Computational Statistics and Data Analytics Lab. He served as a plenary speaker at the 2025 Joint Annual Meetings of the MAA-Florida Section.

  • Computational Neuroscience
  • Machine Learning
  • Working Memory
  • Stochastic Modeling

spu@uwf.edu • shusenpu.com

PC
Co-Founder • Cell Biology

Peter Cavnar, Ph.D.

Associate Professor & Associate Dean • HMCSE

Dr. Cavnar's research sits at the intersection of cell biology and neuroscience, investigating how cytoskeletal regulatory proteins like HAX1 and HS1 govern cell motility and survival, and how dopamine receptor signaling in neutrophils illuminates neuro-immune pathways relevant to psychiatric and neurological disease. He has mentored more than 35 undergraduate and graduate students and serves as co-PI for UWF's NIH U-RISE program. As former Department Chair of Biology and a Howard Hughes Medical Institute Scientific Teaching Fellow, he has reshaped undergraduate biology education at UWF.

  • Cytoskeletal Dynamics
  • Neuro-Immune Interactions
  • Neutrophil Biology
  • Antipsychotic Pharmacology

pcavnar@uwf.edu

DT
Co-Founder • Public Health Neuroscience

Daudet Ilunga Tshiswaka, Ph.D.

Associate Professor, Public Health • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Tshiswaka examines the social and structural forces that drive disparities in stroke mortality, healthcare access, and brain health outcomes — particularly in Black, Hispanic, and immigrant communities. A Visiting Assistant Professor at Columbia University's Stroke Division from 2019–2023 and former Acting Chair of Public Health at UWF, he has led NIH/NINDS-funded research on stroke literacy interventions and health equity across Florida's Panhandle. His work has been published in Stroke (AHA), Preventing Chronic Disease (CDC), and the Journal of Public Health.

  • Stroke Disparities
  • Health Equity
  • Community Health
  • Immigrant Health

dilunga@uwf.edu

VR
Cognitive & Developmental Neuroscience

Vanessa Rainey, Ph.D.

Associate Professor, Psychology • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Rainey investigates sensory processing differences in autism spectrum disorder and ADHD using EEG, examining the neural underpinnings of attention, working memory, and language development. She explores how home environments, bilingualism, and cultural factors shape brain development in children and adolescents. Her work has been featured in Scholarship of Teaching and Learning in Psychology, the International Journal of Psychophysiology, and Psychophysiology. She is co-authoring a new edition of a major human development textbook (MacMillan) and is deeply committed to high-impact undergraduate mentorship.

  • ASD & ADHD
  • EEG/Neuroimaging
  • Sensory Processing
  • Language & Cognition

vrainey@uwf.edu • cogdevlab.uwf.edu

LB
Cognitive Psychology & Applied Cognition

Lisa Blalock, Ph.D.

Professor & Chair, Psychology • Usha Kundu College of Health

Dr. Blalock's research centers on visual working memory — how people store, maintain, and retrieve visual information — and how training can build visual expertise. Using rigorous behavioral experiments, she investigates encoding, maintenance, and retrieval processes with applications ranging from eyewitness testimony to educational design and user interface development. She is a contributor to the Oxford Handbook of Undergraduate Psychology Education (2nd edition) and is known for innovative pedagogical approaches that translate cognitive science into real-world learning strategies.

  • Visual Working Memory
  • Applied Cognition
  • Visual Expertise
  • Educational Psychology

lblalock@uwf.edu

YL
Exercise Neuroscience

Youngil Lee, Ph.D.

Professor, Kinesiology • Hal Marcus College of Science & Engineering

Dr. Lee investigates how endurance exercise protects the brain and heart through autophagy and mitophagy — the cellular processes that clear dysfunctional proteins and organelles. His research demonstrates that regular physical activity can mitigate neurodegenerative diseases including Alzheimer's and Parkinson's, and counteract metabolic-distress-induced senescence in the hippocampus. Published in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise, the Journal of Molecular Neuroscience, and Life Sciences, his work has direct translational implications for aging brain health. He is a 2024–2027 Reubin O'D. Askew Institute for Multidiscipline Studies Fellow.

  • Exercise Neuroprotection
  • Autophagy/Mitophagy
  • Neurodegeneration Prevention
  • Metabolic Disease

ylee@uwf.edu

NeuroNauts Scholars Program

An intensive, NIH-funded summer research experience that brings high school students into working neuroscience laboratories at UWF. This is not a camp — it is real research, in real labs, with real scientists.

NeuroNauts scholars work side-by-side with undergraduate and graduate researchers in one of our six cluster laboratories. They design experiments, collect and analyze data, present findings, and leave with a scientific identity — and often a letter of recommendation — that sets them apart in their college applications.

  • Hands-on research in active NIH-funded laboratories
  • Mentorship from faculty, graduate, and undergraduate researchers
  • Training in EEG, cell culture, computational modeling, behavioral experiments, and community health research methods
  • Weekly journal clubs and scientific communication workshops
  • Final symposium presentation for faculty and family
  • Certificate of completion and faculty letters of support available
